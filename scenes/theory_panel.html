Core ΛCDM Equations
Equation 1
General Friedmann Equation Foundation
Alexander Friedmann (1922) · Einstein Field Equations applied to a homogeneous, isotropic universe

The Friedmann equation is the cosmological master equation governing the expansion rate of the universe. It emerges directly from Einstein's General Relativity field equations applied to the Friedmann-Lemaître-Robertson-Walker (FLRW) metric, which describes a spatially homogeneous and isotropic universe.

The left-hand side is the Hubble parameter H(t) squared — the fractional rate of change of the scale factor. On the right, three distinct physical components compete: ordinary and dark matter density ρ drives gravitational attraction (decelerates expansion), spatial curvature k can help or hinder, and the cosmological constant Λ (dark energy) provides the repulsive force currently dominating the universe's acceleration.

Equation 2
Flat ΛCDM First-Order ODE Solved in This Sim
Simplified Friedmann Eq. for k=0 · Solved numerically by trapezoidal integration over 3000 steps

For the flat universe case (k=0) confirmed by CMB observations, the Friedmann equation simplifies to this first-order ODE. Setting ρ = ρ₀·a⁻³ (matter density dilutes as volume grows) and substituting the density parameters Ωm and ΩΛ gives this elegant operational form.

This is the equation this simulation directly integrates via the trapezoidal method to build the a(t) lookup table. The integration gives us the cosmological time t(a), which is then inverted to a(t) on a uniform time grid, producing a table accurate to <1% compared to Ned Wright's Cosmology Calculator.

Both the matter term (Ωm·a⁻³) and dark energy term (ΩΛ) coexist in the square root — the universe transitions from matter-dominated deceleration to dark-energy-dominated acceleration around t ≈ 7.38 Gyr.

Equation 3
Physical vs. Comoving Coordinates Rendered in Real-Time
FLRW metric decomposition · Core of every Hubble Flow visualization in this simulation

This is the fundamental equation of cosmic expansion made visible by this simulation. Comoving coordinates x are fixed — they move with the cosmic grid, not with the expansion. Physical distances r_physical grow with the scale factor a(t).

When you drag the time slider left (toward the Big Bang), a(t) → 0 and all physical distances shrink toward the origin. Drag right to the present day and a(t) = 1. The blue comoving grid visible in the scene stretches in real-time, demonstrating that it is space itself that expands — not galaxies flying through space.

Key insight: The 25 named star systems are placed at their real comoving coordinates. The 200+ NASA exoplanet host stars are also stored in comoving space. Every frame, all positions are multiplied by a(t) to derive physical positions.

Equation 4
Hubble's Law & Recession Velocity H(t) Readout Live
Edwin Hubble (1929) · Generalized to time-varying H(t) form by ΛCDM theory

Hubble's Law states that every galaxy in the universe appears to recede from us at a speed proportional to its distance. The constant of proportionality is the Hubble parameter H(t). Today, H(t₀) = H₀ = 67.4 km/s/Mpc — meaning a galaxy 1 Megaparsec away recedes at 67.4 km/s; at 10 Mpc it recedes at 674 km/s.

Notice that H(t) is not a constant — it evolves as the universe's composition changes. In the matter-dominated era H(t) was much larger; dark energy is gradually making H(t) approach a constant again (the de Sitter limit). The simulation displays the live H(t) value in the control bar as you scrub through time.

Numerical Implementation
How a(t) is computed in this simulation

Step 1 — Build t(a): Integrate the Friedmann ODE using the trapezoidal rule over 3000 steps from a=0.001 to a=1.5. Each step computes dt = da / (a·H₀·√(Ωm·a⁻³ + ΩΛ)).

Step 2 — Invert to a(t): The (a, t) pairs are linearly interpolated to produce 3001 uniformly-spaced (t, a) entries from t=0.1 to t=14.0 Gyr. This table is serialised as JSON and injected into the Three.js scene.

Step 3 — Runtime lookup: Each slider tick triggers a binary search on the table, retrieving a(t), z, and H(t) in O(log n) time. All 200+ star positions are then updated via r = a(t) × x_comoving in a single JavaScript typed-array loop.

Verification: The computed t(a=1) matches the Ned Wright Cosmology Calculator within <1.25%, and the Hubble parameter H₀=67.4 km/s/Mpc is reproduced exactly at t₀.
